
H1 Conflict with your partner or your teen’s other parent? Leave your teenager out of it
Managing conflict with your partner or your teen’s other parent is just as important as managing conflict with your teenager. Even if you have separated from your teen’s other parent, it’s important for you to manage conflict in order to protect your teen’s mental health.
When you have disagreements with your partner or your teen’s other parent...

H3 Do not ask your teenager to choose sides.
It’s important for your teenager to have a positive relationship with both parents. Asking them to choose sides makes this difficult for them.

H3 Do not bad mouth them to your teenager.
If you get frustrated with your partner or your teen’s other parent, it’s better to say which specific behaviour is upsetting you rather than criticising them in a personal way.

H3 Find a private place away from your teenager.
Seeing frequent and intense conflict between parents is upsetting for teenagers.

H3 Try to be on the same page about parenting during the pandemic.
Communicate regularly with your partner or teen’s other parent about your parenting approach during the pandemic (e.g. expectations for your teen about following the local restrictions). Discuss expectations about sharing parenting responsibilities, including how this might change if local restrictions change.
[IMAGE: 20.svg - child on tightrope]
Consider seeking support from a trusted friend or professional if you are struggling to manage conflict or family relationships, including conflict with your teen, your partner, or co-parent.
This is especially important if you’ve noticed increased conflict during the pandemic. Managing your conflicts well is important not only for your own well-being but also for your teenager’s.
