
H2 Having the conversations
Supporting your teen through the pandemic starts by acknowledging the elephant in the room. Even though we’ve been dealing with pandemic for some time now, it’s important to keep the conversations going. Constantly changing local regulations and restrictions can make your teen more anxious. You can help your teen feel more in control by providing them with clear, up-to-date and age-appropriate information about the pandemic and restrictions when the situation changes.
Talking with your teen can also help you understand what’s on their mind and how they are coping.
So where do you start?
One way is to think of it like cooking. Start by having all the right ingredients, and make all the preparations before you heat up the pan.
[IMAGE: ingredients.svg]
1. Reliable and up-to-date resources about the pandemic
2. Understanding of your child’s current development needs and knowledge about the pandemic
3. Coping strategies to manage your own worries. It’s important to manage your own anxiety before talking with your teen, so that you don’t reinforce your teen’s anxiety. Try to use helpful coping strategies that work for you.

Once you’ve prepared the ingredients, it’s time to start cooking!
First, plan how you want to discuss the pandemic with your teen. Adapt when and how much you talk with your teen based on their needs, for example if you notice them feeling more anxious, or when the local situation changes.
Here are some tips to keep in mind when talking to your teen about COVID-related topics.
[IMAGE: instructions.svg]
DO's
- Use a calm and reassuring tone. Even if you feel anxious yourself, try to model being calm.
- Begin by asking what your teen already knows and whether they have any questions. Adapt the conversation based on what they already know and what they want to know.
- Ask what worries them most about the pandemic  (e.g., risk of infection, impacts of local  restrictions, global effect of the pandemic)
- Talk about the facts of the pandemic in a way that best suits your teen’s personality, e.g. if they are prone to anxiety or to disregard rules.
- Be prepared to repeat information multiple times. It may be hard for your teen to understand or accept new information initially.
DONT's
- Avoid lecturing, being overly anxious, catastrophising, or focusing on worst-case scenarios to make an impact.  Instead, provide honest and accurate information.
- Refrain from discussing information that is not appropriate to their age, including things they are unlikely to understand, or that may increase their anxiety (e.g. family financial difficulties).
- Don’t end the conversation when your child is upset - keep talking through what’s upsetting them and help them to calm down.
