
H2 So how can you best support your teen if a loved one dies?

H5 Click here for some tips:
- Let your teen know you will be there for them when they are ready to talk. Make yourself available when they do want to talk with you. Focus on listening and empathising.
- Let them know that it is okay to feel however they feel (e.g. sadness, overwhelm, anger, worry, separation anxiety) and that there is no right or wrong way to grieve.
- Give them time and space to grieve in ways that work for them (e.g. allowing them to have a space in the house where they can have some time to themselves).
- Regularly check in with them. Ask how they feel, what they are thinking about, how they are coping, and whether they have any questions.
- Help them find ways to express themselves (e.g. journaling, drawing, playing music).
- Help them find ways to honour the death of their loved one while following the local regulations. You might need to be creative - e.g. light a candle, write a letter, plant a flower or tree.
- If your teen was unable to see the loved one before they died, assure them that this was out of their control.
Grief is a normal process that usually reduces in intensity over time. However, if you think your teen’s reaction is particularly intense and/or persistent (e.g. major impact on everyday functioning, not improving over time), consider seeking professional mental health support.
